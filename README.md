## 1. SHORT BORROW FEE RATE: 17.86% (Annualized)

### What This Means

The short borrow fee is the **cost per year** that a short seller pays to borrow shares. A 17.86% rate is extraordinarily high for any stock.

**Comparative context:**
- **Easy-to-borrow stocks (standard):** < 1.0% (Amazon, Apple, SPY, etc.)
- **Moderately tight stocks:** 2–5% (some meme stocks historically)
- **Very scarce stocks:** 10–30% (extreme squeeze candidates)
- **AIBT:** **17.86%** — locked in this range since at least June 3, 2026

### Mechanics: Why This Matters

At 17.86%, a short seller carrying a 1 million-share position pays **$178,600 per year** in borrow fees alone — roughly **$490/day** in carrying costs. For a stock trading in the sub-penny range ($0.0010), this creates **massive daily bleeding** on underwater positions.

**This fee reflects broker consensus: shares are genuinely scarce.** Brokers don't arbitrarily raise fees — they respond to the balance of lendable shares vs. demand from short sellers. A sustained 17.86% rate over weeks indicates:

1. **Persistent demand to short** — people keep wanting to establish or hold short positions despite the cost
2. **Limited lendable inventory** — the supply of shares brokers can locate for lending is constrained
3. **Low probability shorts will exit voluntarily** — at $490/day in fees on a fractional-cent stock, shorts are likely underwater and *can't* cover without realizing massive losses

### Historical Analogs

- **GameStop (GME) during Jan 2021 squeeze:** Borrow rates peaked at 50%+
- **AMC during peak squeeze:** Rates exceeded 100% annualized at extreme points
- **VW squeeze 2008:** Borrow rates spiked to 90%+ as the squeeze compressed

A 17.86% rate on an OTC micro-cap *is* the equivalent of multi-digit rates on a major exchange stock — the scarcity signal is even sharper because OTC share inventories are already thinner to begin with.

### Current Trajectory

The rate has held **flat and stable** since June 3, suggesting this isn't a temporary spike but **entrenched scarcity**. Shorts are committed and bleeding; they haven't panicked-covered yet.

## 3. FAILS-TO-DELIVER (FTD): 2.01M SHARES (PEAK, 6/4/26)

### What This Means

An FTD occurs when a short seller sells shares but **cannot locate and deliver them by settlement** (typically T+2, meaning 2 business days after sale). A cumulative FTD count measures shares that have failed delivery and remained unsettled.

**AIBT FTD history (last 12 months):**

| Date | FTD Shares | Context |
|---|---|---|
| 2026-06-04 | 2,011,500 | **Peak** |
| 2026-05-04 | 1,799,266 | Sustained high |
| 2026-05-01 | 1,570,377 | Multi-million threshold |
| 2026-05-20 | 699,596 | Elevated |
| 2026-03-27 to 2026-03-26 | ~460K | Consistent |

### Why FTD Spikes Signal Squeeze Risk

An FTD happens when:
1. A short seller sells shares they haven't yet borrowed
2. The lender (or broker) can't find shares to deliver by settlement
3. The position stays "naked" — unsettled, creating a forced-buy obligation eventually

**When FTDs accumulate to millions of shares on a stock with limited float, it creates a queue of forced buy-backs.** The SEC and brokers don't allow infinite FTD periods; they eventually force settlement, which means:

- **Shorts *must* buy back shares** to close the failed position
- On a tightly-supplied stock, buying millions of shares drives price up violently
- Shorts covering forced buys trigger margin calls on other shorts
- **Cascade effect:** forced covering triggers more forced covering

### AIBT FTD Context

With a **565.92M float**, 2M FTD shares is ~**0.35% of float**. On a mega-cap that's negligible. But on an OTC pink sheet with daily volumes in the 1–20M range, a 2M FTD backlog represents:

- **2–20 days of total trading volume** (depending on the day)
- Equivalent to **10–100% of average daily volume** having failed settlement
- A genuine queue of forced buy obligations

The **pattern of repeated 500K–2M FTD spikes** over the past 12 months shows this is chronic, not a one-time glitch.

**Zero availability has occurred at least 3 times in 7 months.** The most recent instance was **June 10, 2026** — just 3 weeks before the current analysis.

### Squeeze Mechanics: The Dry Well

When shortable share availability reaches zero:

1. **New shorts cannot easily establish positions** — anyone wanting to short has to wait for a locate or pay a premium
2. **Existing shorts cannot cover** (they can't find shares to buy back efficiently)
3. **The borrow fee becomes a true pain point** — shorts are trapped, paying 17.86% annually with no exit path
4. **Retail demand can move the needle dramatically** — if 1M retail investors decide to buy, there's no borrow supply to dampen that demand via short-selling

This is the **tightest supply condition possible**. It's the precondition for explosive upside moves.

### Historical Parallel

During the January 2021 GME squeeze, shortable share availability repeatedly hit zero or single-digit numbers. On days when availability was zero and volume spiked, GME moved **+50% to +100% intraday**. The mechanism:

- Shorts couldn't short the rise (no shares)
